# PIP — Voter Base Profile

## Who Votes PIP

### Historical Core: The Intellectual Independence Tradition
For decades, PIP's base was the Puerto Rican intellectual and cultural elite: university professors, writers, lawyers, artists. These are people who vote PIP as a principled stance even knowing the party won't win — a political statement about who they are and what Puerto Rico should be.

### The 2020 Expansion: Young Progressives
Dalmau's 2020 campaign brought a new demographic: young Puerto Ricans who are:
- Educated and economically precarious (high student debt, low local wages)
- Skeptical of both PNP and PPD
- Attracted to Dalmau's economic arguments (debt cancellation, anti-PROMESA)
- Influenced by U.S. progressive politics (Bernie Sanders, AOC) — AOC is Puerto Rican and openly pro-independence
- Active on social media where independence arguments circulate freely

### Geographic Distribution
- **University towns:** UPR Río Piedras, UPR Arecibo, UPR Mayagüez campuses — faculty and students
- **San Juan/Santurce:** Urban progressive core
- **Historically:** Some presence in western PR (cultural tradition)
- **Diaspora:** Puerto Ricans in New York, Chicago, Massachusetts — many diaspora Puerto Ricans are more independence-leaning than island residents (economic independence fears are less salient for those living in the U.S.)

### Occupation
- University faculty and researchers
- Lawyers and civil rights advocates
- Artists, musicians, writers
- Journalists and media workers
- NGO and social advocacy workers
- Some medical professionals
- Young workers with economic precarity and political anger

## The Psychology of PIP Voting

### Principled Over Pragmatic
PIP voters knowingly vote for a party that won't win. This requires a particular psychology:
- "I vote my conscience, not my fear"
- "The two parties have governed us for 80 years and made us poorer and more dependent — why would I vote for them again?"
- "My vote is a statement about who we are and who we could be"
- The independence vote as an act of cultural self-assertion

### The Diaspora Experience
Many diaspora Puerto Ricans who become independence supporters were radicalized by experiencing American racism and discrimination on the mainland. They expected full inclusion as U.S. citizens and found instead that their Puerto Rican identity was a social liability. This experience converts many to independence thinking: "If American citizenship doesn't protect us from discrimination, what's the value of this relationship?"

### The Historical Justice Argument
"We did not choose colonialism. It was imposed on us in 1898 when the U.S. took Puerto Rico from Spain as war booty, without asking Puerto Ricans. We deserve the right to choose our own future. That is a matter of justice, regardless of economic calculation."

## What Moves PIP Voters

1. Historical justice / decolonization framing
2. Economic sovereignty arguments (PROMESA as colonial imposition)
3. Cultural identity and pride in Puerto Rican nationhood
4. Anti-corruption and system critique
5. International solidarity (Puerto Rico in the global context of decolonization)
6. Albizu Campos and the resistance tradition
7. Specific candidate charisma (Dalmau has this; previous PIP leaders often lacked mass appeal)
