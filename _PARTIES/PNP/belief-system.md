# PNP — Belief System & Ideology

## Core Identity
The PNP's foundational and defining ideology is **estadidad** — the pursuit of statehood for Puerto Rico within the United States. Every other position flows from or is colored by this central commitment.

---

## The Statehood Doctrine

### What They Believe
- Puerto Rico should be the 51st state of the United States
- As a state, Puerto Ricans would have full voting representation in Congress
- Statehood would bring federal funding parity (Medicare, Medicaid, SSI at same rates as states)
- Statehood is framed as a matter of **equality** and **dignity** — not just political convenience
- U.S. citizenship of Puerto Ricans should be fully expressed through full democratic participation

### The Argument
PNP argues that Puerto Ricans are U.S. citizens who pay federal taxes, serve in the military at high rates, and yet have no voting representation in Congress and no presidential vote. This is framed as a democratic deficit and a form of colonialism that only statehood can resolve.

### Internal Tensions on Status
- **Hard-liners:** Statehood or nothing — refuse any ELA enhancement
- **Pragmatists:** Accept a multi-step process toward statehood
- Some PNP members are culturally uncomfortable with full Americanization but politically committed to statehood for economic reasons

---

## Economic Ideology

### Core Positions
- **Pro-business:** Supports private sector investment, corporate tax incentives
- **Pro-privatization (historically):** Under Rosselló in the 1990s, sold public telephone company, privatized some services
- **Pro-Act 60 (formerly Act 20/22):** Supports tax incentives for U.S. investors relocating to PR
- **Fiscal conservatism:** Generally supports balanced budgets, debt reduction
- **Federal parity:** Advocates for equal federal funding as states receive (Medicaid, SSI, food stamps)

### PROMESA / Fiscal Board
- Officially opposes the Fiscal Oversight Board (imposed by U.S. Congress in 2016)
- But navigates it pragmatically — cannot ignore it
- Jenniffer González was the Resident Commissioner who supported PROMESA initially but later criticized the Board's overreach

### Infrastructure
- LUMA Energy (private electricity contractor): Generally supported by PNP administration
- PRASA (water): Public, but PNP has historically accepted public-private partnerships

## Social & Cultural Ideology

### Traditional Values
- PNP has historically been moderate-conservative on social issues
- Strong Catholic base in the party
- On LGBTQ+ rights: Generally accepts established law but doesn't champion it; some members openly oppose
- On abortion: Divided — party doesn't have a unified position; follows U.S. Republican trends loosely

### Language
- Historically comfortable with bilingualism (Spanish AND English)
- Some tensions: English-official bill controversies in 1990s
- Current position: Spanish remains primary but English officialization is a statehood goal

### Cultural Identity
- PNP politicians strongly assert Puerto Rican cultural identity while pursuing statehood
- "We can be Puerto Rican AND American" is the rhetorical reconciliation
- Criticism from opponents: Statehood will lead to cultural assimilation

---

## Governance Philosophy

- Strong executive governance
- Centralized party discipline
- Historically: large government modernization projects, public works
- Privatization in 1990s shifted this somewhat
- Under Jenniffer González: More institutionalist, works within federal system effectively

---

## Rhetorical Patterns & Messaging

### Key Words
"Igualdad" · "Dignidad" · "Ciudadanía Plena" · "Progreso" · "Estadidad" · "Trabajo" · "Familia"

### Emotional Frame
- Pride in being American AND Puerto Rican
- Frustration with second-class treatment by federal government
- Optimism about Puerto Rico's potential as a state
- Law-and-order undertone (strong on security)

### What PNP Politicians NEVER Say
- Anything that sounds anti-American
- Criticism of U.S. military
- Independence-adjacent language
- "Colonia" (they reject this framing)
