# MVC — Party History 2019–2026

## The Context: El Verano del 19 (Summer of 2019)

MVC cannot be understood without the mass protests of July 2019. When 916 pages of private chat messages by Governor Ricardo Rosselló were leaked, showing systematic mockery of hurricane victims, racist jokes, misogynistic language, and casual references to corruption, Puerto Rico exploded.

For approximately two weeks, hundreds of thousands of Puerto Ricans took to the streets — unprecedented in PR history. The protests were spontaneous, self-organized, and came from across political affiliations. On August 2, 2019, Rosselló resigned — the first Puerto Rico governor to resign from office.

**The Verano del 19 created MVC's founding political opportunity:**
- It demonstrated that mass mobilization was possible
- It showed that the PNP/PPD duopoly was not inevitable
- It energized a generation of young activists who wanted political change
- It gave political credibility to voices outside the traditional party system

## Founding (2019)

Following the protests, several existing political and community figures came together to formalize MVC as a political party. Key founding figures:
- **Manuel Natal Albelo:** Former PPD Representative who had grown frustrated with PPD's ideological limitations
- **Community organizers:** Legal advocates, environmental activists, housing rights workers
- **Alexandra Lúgaro:** Independent candidate who had shown strong showings in 2016 (nearly 11% for governor as independent)

The party registered formally and prepared for the 2020 election cycle.

## First Election: 2020

MVC's 2020 debut exceeded expectations:
- Lúgaro ran for governor: approximately 14% of vote (second highest for non-PNP/PPD candidate ever)
- Won 2 Senate seats and 2 House seats
- Mariana Nogales elected to House por acumulación
- José Vargas Vidot elected to Senate (initially as independent, affiliated with MVC)
- This breakthrough made MVC the third force in PR politics

**What made 2020 work:**
- Post-Telegramgate energy still active
- COVID-19 pandemic exposed government failures
- Social media organizing replaced traditional events (COVID restricted events)
- Clear, principled messaging on multiple issues

## Growth and Consolidation (2021–2023)

- MVC legislators built profile through active participation in legislative sessions
- Nogales became viral for her detailed explanations of legislative issues
- Special elections in 2022 tested MVC's organizational capacity
- Alliance conversations with PIP began

## The Alianza de País and 2024

- MVC and PIP formalized Alianza de País for 2024 elections
- Juan Dalmau (PIP) ran as Alianza gubernatorial candidate
- Result: Additional seats through Constitutional Minority Clause (activated by PNP supermajority)
- MVC/Alianza now has increased legislative presence
- The alliance model is likely to continue for 2028

## Current Status (2026)

MVC is established as PR's third major political force:
- Has legislative presence in both chambers
- Mariana Nogales is one of PR's most recognizable political figures
- Building organizational infrastructure for 2028
- Debates internally about status position (more independence-aligned than MVC officially states)
- Growing tension between being a movement and being a party
