# PPD — Belief System & Ideology

## Core Identity
The PPD's identity is built on three pillars: the **Estado Libre Asociado** (Commonwealth), **Puerto Rican cultural sovereignty**, and a **center-left social democracy** tradition. It is the party of pragmatic nationalism — maintaining a distinct Puerto Rican identity within the U.S. framework.

---

## The Commonwealth Doctrine

### What They Believe
- Puerto Rico's current Commonwealth relationship with the U.S. should be maintained and enhanced
- The ELA represents a unique, creative solution to the colonial question that protects PR's culture while maintaining U.S. economic ties
- Puerto Rico should have **more autonomy** within the U.S. framework ("ELA Soberano" or "ELA Digno" faction)
- Full independence is too economically risky; statehood risks cultural assimilation

### The Argument
- Puerto Rico is a distinct nation — different language, culture, history — and must preserve this identity
- The economic relationship with the U.S. provides essential benefits (federal programs, trade, currency)
- A creative enhanced Commonwealth is the only solution that honors both identity and economic reality

### Internal Crisis on Status
The PPD's status position has become increasingly untenable:
- Multiple plebiscites have shown growing support for statehood
- Young PPD voters don't feel strongly about ELA
- "ELA Soberano" (some independence-adjacent) vs. status quo PPD is an ongoing internal debate
- **The PPD has pivoted away from status** — recent campaigns emphasize governance, economy, local issues over status

---

## Economic Ideology

### Core Positions
- **Center-left economics:** Historically pro-labor, pro-public sector, pro-social programs
- **Anti-austerity (recent):** Opposes PROMESA Fiscal Board's cuts to pensions, public services
- **Pro-federal investment:** Advocates for Medicaid parity, food stamp parity, SSI parity
- **Anti-privatization (current):** Strongly opposes LUMA Energy; has called for PREPA to be restored to public operation
- **Pro-union:** Strong union support, especially public sector
- **Municipal-focused economics:** PPD mayors are the implementers of economic programs at local level

### Operation Bootstrap Legacy
- The PPD's industrial policy (1950s–1970s) created Puerto Rico's manufacturing economy
- Federal tax exemption (Section 936) that powered this was eliminated in 1996 — PPD blames this for economic decline
- This creates a narrative: "PPD built PR's prosperity, Congress (with PNP backing) destroyed it"

## Social & Cultural Ideology

### Cultural Nationalism
- The PPD is the guardian of Puerto Rican culture, language, and identity
- Spanish is the language of Puerto Rico and must be protected
- "La Pava" (the jibaro hat) symbolizes the rural, working-class, authentic Puerto Rican
- PPD frames itself as the defender of lo puertorriqueño against Americanization

### Social Positions
- More socially moderate than MVC/PIP on LGBTQ+ issues
- Divided on abortion (Catholic base is large)
- Strong labor and workers' rights positions
- Community organizing tradition — barrio-level political machinery

### Religion
- Historically Catholic-dominant base
- Rural Catholic community is PPD's core
- Evangelical growth in PR has pulled some traditional PPD voters toward PD

---

## The PPD Machine — Municipal Power

The PPD's greatest asset is its **municipal political machine**:
- 41 mayors (2024) means control of 41 municipal governments
- Municipal governments control thousands of jobs, contracts, services
- PPD mayors are the face of government for most rural Puerto Ricans
- This creates a patronage network that survives legislative defeats
- PPD mayors are often more popular than the party itself

**Key PPD mayoral strongholds:**
- Traditionally: Interior municipalities (Lares, Adjuntas, Jayuya, etc.)
- Many western and mountainous municipalities
- Some northern municipalities

## Rhetorical Patterns

### Key Words
"Pueblo" · "Identidad" · "Familia puertorriqueña" · "La Pava" · "Soberanía" · "Lo nuestro" · "Los que más necesitan"

### Emotional Frame
- Pride and nostalgia for Puerto Rican identity and culture
- Protector of the vulnerable, the worker, the poor
- Suspicion of corporations, privatization, outsiders
- "El pueblo" as the highest value

### Anti-Frames
- Against "los ricos" (the wealthy taking advantage)
- Against privatization as "selling Puerto Rico"
- Against statehood as "assimilation" and loss of identity
