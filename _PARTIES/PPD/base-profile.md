# PPD — Voter Base Profile

## Who Votes PPD

### Core Traditional Base

**Rural Puerto Rico:** The PPD's deepest roots are in interior mountain municipalities — Lares, Adjuntas, Jayuya, Utuado, Maricao, San Sebastián. These communities have historical ties to the PPD going back to the Muñoz Marín era. The party's agrarian reform, roads, and electrification programs built loyalty that spans generations.

**Public Sector Workers:** Government employees, public school teachers, municipal workers — historically PPD-aligned. Public unions tend to support PPD over PNP (which is more likely to privatize). The government remains Puerto Rico's largest employer.

**Labor Unions:** PPD has traditional ties to labor unions — sugar workers historically, now public sector unions, healthcare workers, teachers' unions.

**Rural Catholic Communities:** The jibaro (rural Puerto Rican) identity is PPD's cultural core. Small-town parish-centered communities. Social conservatism but economic populism.

### Geographic Profile
**PPD strongholds:**
- Interior mountain municipalities (the "cordillera central" belt)
- Western PR: Mayagüez area, Cabo Rojo, San Germán
- Some northern municipalities: Camuy, Quebradillas, parts of the Arecibo region
- Some eastern municipalities

**41 PPD mayors in 2024** covers a diverse range — from small mountain towns to larger western cities.

### Age Profile
PPD skews older. The generation that grew up with Muñoz Marín's transformative impact and feels genuine loyalty to the ELA is aging out. Younger voters are more likely to choose PNP (if status-oriented) or MVC (if progressive).

### Declining Competitiveness with Youth
Young Puerto Ricans increasingly choose MVC/PIP for progressive politics or PNP for economic integration. PPD's ELA position seems outdated to many under-40 voters who didn't grow up in the era when ELA was a revolutionary achievement.

## What Drives PPD Loyalty

### Identity Attachment
"La Pava" (the jibaro straw hat) is one of the most powerful political symbols in PR history. For older PPD voters, it represents who they are — working-class, rural, authentically Puerto Rican, not defined by American identity. This is cultural, not purely political.

### Fear of Statehood
Many PPD voters genuinely fear that statehood means cultural assimilation — loss of Spanish, loss of Puerto Rican identity, becoming just another state. This fear is PPD's most reliable emotional lever.

### Historical Gratitude
Muñoz Marín's programs genuinely transformed Puerto Rican lives. Grandparents who got land through agrarian reform, who saw roads and schools built — their descendants remember. This loyalty persists even as the party's current program is less compelling.

### Anti-Privatization Sentiment
Public sector workers fear PNP's privatization tendencies. The LUMA Energy experience (hated by most Puerto Ricans) energizes this sentiment: "PNP sold our electricity system to a private company that raised rates and kept the lights off."

### The Resident Commissioner as Hope
Pablo Hernández Rivera's 2024 win gives PPD a tangible asset — a Washington voice. This matters to federal employees, federal program recipients, and those who care about PR's relationship with Congress.
