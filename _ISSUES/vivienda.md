# Issue Intelligence: Vivienda (Housing)

## Overview
Housing in Puerto Rico is a multifaceted crisis involving: affordability pressures from wealthy investors, displacement of long-term residents, post-Maria reconstruction still incomplete, homelessness at record levels, and aging public housing infrastructure. It is rapidly becoming one of the most politically salient issues especially for urban legislators.

---

## The Act 60 / Investor Displacement Crisis

### The Law
Act 60 of 2019 (consolidating earlier Act 20 and Act 22) provides extraordinary tax incentives:
- **Individual investors (Chapter 2 — formerly Act 22):** Puerto Rican residents who move to PR from the U.S. mainland pay **0% capital gains tax** on PR-sourced income and gains
- **Export services companies (Chapter 3 — formerly Act 20):** 4% corporate tax rate (vs. 21% federal rate for mainland companies)
- **Requirement:** Must spend at least 183 days per year in Puerto Rico; must make charitable contributions

### Who's Coming
- Wealthy U.S. investors, particularly from high-tax states (California, New York)
- Crypto/tech entrepreneurs seeking tax advantages
- Real estate investors
- Day traders and capital gains-heavy investors
- Estimates: 5,000–15,000 Act 22/60 beneficiaries (exact numbers disputed)

### The Displacement Effect

**Santurce, Condado, Ocean Park (San Juan):**
- These historically working and middle-class neighborhoods have experienced dramatic rent increases
- Airbnb and short-term rental conversion: long-term rental units converted to tourist accommodations
- Building purchases by investors renovating for wealthy tenants or short-term rental
- Long-term Puerto Rican residents forced out due to rent increases or landlord decisions to convert
- Documented cases: Puerto Rican families evicted from homes their families had lived in for generations

**The Cultural Argument:**
Displacement of Puerto Ricans from their neighborhoods is not just economic — it's cultural. Communities in Santurce had distinct cultural identities (Afro-Puerto Rican history in parts, working-class art scene, multigenerational families). Act 60 investors arriving en masse can change community character.

**The Counter-Argument (PNP):**
- Investment brings economic activity, construction jobs, business income
- New restaurants, shops, and services benefit local workers
- Some Puerto Ricans benefit from higher property values (if they own)
- Tax revenue generated by investors funds some public services

### Political Positions on Act 60

| Party | Position | Key Argument |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| PNP | Support | Economic development, investment attraction |
| PPD | Critical | Unfair to Puerto Ricans, displacement |
| MVC | Strongly oppose | Displacement is a human rights issue |
| PIP | Strongly oppose | Colonial investment pattern |
| PD | Support | Free market, private investment |

## Short-Term Rentals (Airbnb) Crisis

- Airbnb and similar platforms have reduced the long-term rental housing supply in tourist-attractive areas
- San Juan's beach neighborhoods (Isla Verde, Condado, Ocean Park, Santurce) most affected
- Rincón, Vieques, and other tourist areas also significantly affected
- Puerto Rico's government has struggled to regulate short-term rentals effectively
- MVC has been most aggressive in pushing for stronger regulations

---

## Post-Maria Housing Crisis

### The Destruction
- Hurricane Maria destroyed or severely damaged an estimated 300,000 homes
- Many families with blue tarps (FEMA temporary roofing) for months or years
- Some communities in mountainous interior had limited reconstruction even years later

### Federal Recovery Funds (CDBG-DR)
- Community Development Block Grant — Disaster Recovery (CDBG-DR) funds allocated to PR: over $20 billion
- **Slow disbursement:** Puerto Rico's bureaucratic capacity and federal oversight requirements created years of delay
- As of 2025-2026: Funds still being disbursed; some reconstruction programs still ongoing 8+ years after Maria
- **R3 Program:** PR's main housing rehabilitation program using CDBG-DR; has helped thousands of families but also faced significant criticism for delays and fraud

---

## Public Housing (Caseríos)

### Overview
- Puerto Rico has one of the densest public housing systems in the U.S. territory system
- **Caseríos** (public housing projects) are community anchors in many urban areas
- Residents: Predominantly low-income; high unemployment; historical PPD political base
- Infrastructure: Many caseríos have aging, deteriorating buildings

### Social Dynamics
- Gang activity is concentrated in some caseríos — residents are often victims, not perpetrators
- Community organizations within caseríos have historically been important civic institutions
- HOPE VI / mixed-income development: Some caseríos have been redeveloped as mixed-income housing (San Juan, Bayamón) with mixed community reception

---

## Homelessness — José Vargas Vidot's Issue

- Puerto Rico has one of the highest homeless population rates in the U.S. jurisdiction system
- San Juan's urban core has a highly visible homeless population
- **Causes:** Drug addiction, mental illness, family breakdown, housing costs, economic crisis
- **Iniciativa Comunitaria** (Vargas Vidot's NGO before entering politics) was a major provider of homeless services
- **Political treatment:** Most politicians treat homelessness as an aesthetics problem (cleaning up visible homeless camps) rather than a systemic issue
- Vargas Vidot is the exception: advocates for housing-first approach, treating addiction as health issue
