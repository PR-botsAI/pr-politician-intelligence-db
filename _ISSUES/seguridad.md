# Issue Intelligence: Seguridad (Public Safety)

## Overview
Public safety is consistently among the top 3 concerns for Puerto Rico residents. PR's crime rate — particularly its murder rate — has been one of the highest in U.S. territory and exceeds most U.S. states. However, crime is not uniform: it is concentrated geographically and demographically, and the experience of crime varies dramatically between communities.

---

## Puerto Rico Crime Reality

### Murder Rate
- Puerto Rico's murder rate has historically been 15–25 per 100,000 people — 3–5 times the U.S. average
- Peak years: 2011 (1,136 murders), 2012 — among the worst years
- Trend: Has declined from peak but remains high
- Context: Most murders are gang-related or drug trafficking-related — they are not random

### Drug Trafficking
**Puerto Rico as transshipment hub:**
- PR's geographic position (between South America and the U.S. mainland) makes it a major transshipment point for cocaine and other drugs moving from Colombia/Venezuela to the continental U.S.
- The Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) has had significant presence in PR for decades
- Puerto Rico's extensive coastline (501 miles) and many small ports/coves make interdiction difficult
- Drug trafficking organizations (DTOs) operate in PR; some with connections to Dominican Republic-based organizations

### Gang Dynamics
- Gangs (pandillas) control specific housing project territories (caseríos) and urban sectors
- Gang violence is the primary driver of PR's murder rate
- Drug distribution is the primary gang economic activity
- Gang membership is often intergenerational in poor urban communities with few economic alternatives

### Geographic Concentration
- Most violent crime is concentrated in:
  - Public housing projects (caseríos) in urban areas
  - Specific neighborhoods in San Juan, Bayamón, Carolina, Ponce
  - Some rural areas with drug trafficking routes
- Middle-class and rural communities have much lower violent crime rates
- Tourists are rarely targeted in violent crimes

## Policía de Puerto Rico (PRPD) — The Institution

### Background
- Puerto Rico has its own police force — the Policía de Puerto Rico (PRPD), separate from FBI
- Historically one of the largest per-capita police forces in the U.S. jurisdiction
- Has faced severe challenges: corruption, brutality complaints, inadequate training

### The Federal Consent Decree (2013–2023)
- In 2013, the U.S. Department of Justice found that PRPD engaged in a pattern of excessive force, unlawful searches, and discriminatory policing
- A federal consent decree was imposed requiring systematic reforms
- PRPD operated under federal supervision for a decade
- The consent decree was formally ended in 2023, with mixed assessments of whether genuine reform occurred

### Staffing Crisis
- PRPD has faced severe recruitment and retention challenges
- Salaries are low relative to mainland U.S. police forces
- Many PRPD officers have left for better-paying mainland positions
- The force is understaffed relative to the crime challenges

### Corruption
- Several PRPD officers have faced federal charges for drug trafficking, bribery, civil rights violations
- Corruption is a persistent problem that undermines public trust

---

## Federal Law Enforcement in Puerto Rico

- **FBI:** Significant PR office; investigates public corruption, drug trafficking, civil rights violations
- **DEA:** Major presence; drug trafficking investigations
- **U.S. Marshals:** Federal fugitive operations
- **CBP (Customs and Border Protection):** Maritime interdiction
- **ATF:** Firearms trafficking
- **ICE:** Immigration enforcement (politically sensitive in PR)

The federal law enforcement presence is significant and more active in PR than in most comparable U.S. jurisdictions, due to the transshipment role and public corruption investigations.

Security resonates differently by politician:
- **PNP politicians:** Emphasize law and order, police support, crime statistics going down

- **MVC politicians:** Emphasize community safety vs. policing, root cause investment
