# Issue Intelligence: Energía (Electricity Crisis)

## Overview
Puerto Rico's electricity crisis is arguably the most universally felt quality-of-life issue on the island. High bills, frequent outages, and the controversial LUMA Energy privatization have made electricity a dominant political issue that affects every politician and every constituent.

---

## PREPA: The Old Puerto Rico Electric Power Authority

### History
- **PREPA** (Autoridad de Energía Eléctrica — AEE) was the public electric utility of Puerto Rico for decades
- One of the largest public utilities in the U.S. territorial system
- Operated power generation, transmission, and distribution
- Built PR's electricity system from near-zero in the 1940s
- Chronically underfunded and politically influenced: fuel oil contracts, patronage employment, deferred maintenance

### PREPA's Pre-Maria Problems
- Over-reliance on petroleum (expensive, volatile prices)
- Aging infrastructure: some generation units were 30–40 years old
- $9 billion in debt by 2017
- Political interference in management
- Filed for bankruptcy in 2017 (Title III under PROMESA)

---

## Hurricane Maria (2017): The Catastrophic Collapse

Hurricane Maria destroyed approximately 80% of Puerto Rico's electrical grid:
- Transmission towers toppled; distribution lines destroyed
- Generation facilities damaged
- The recovery was the largest electricity restoration project in U.S. history
- But it was also one of the slowest:
  - Humacao area: 11 months without power
  - Remote mountain communities: similar timelines
  - Total restoration: over a year for full island
- **The aftermath exposed** just how decrepit and under-maintained the grid was before Maria

## LUMA Energy: The Controversial Privatization

### What Is LUMA?
- A consortium of ATCO (Canadian) and Quanta Services (U.S.)
- Won a 15-year contract to operate Puerto Rico's electricity **transmission and distribution** system
- Contract signed June 22, 2020; LUMA took operational control June 1, 2021
- **Does NOT own the system** — manages it under contract
- Contract value: LUMA receives a management fee; the system assets remain public

### Why LUMA Was Controversial From Day One
- The contract was approved during a public health emergency (COVID-19 pandemic) — limited public input
- Labor: PREPA's unionized workforce was concerned about job losses and contract terms
- Labor Action: PREPA workers struck against LUMA in 2021
- Fees: LUMA's management fee adds to electricity costs
- Experience: Some critics questioned LUMA's experience with a utility of PR's complexity

### LUMA's Performance (2021–2026)

**Negatives (what Puerto Ricans experience):**
- Bills increased significantly (30–60% increases for many customers)
- Outages remained frequent — not significantly better than PREPA era
- Customer service problems
- Vegetation management issues (trees causing outages)
- Hurricane Fiona (September 2022): widespread outages again; LUMA response criticized

**LUMA's arguments:**
- Inherited a deteriorated system that takes years to fix
- Post-Maria reconstruction was incomplete when they took over
- Significant infrastructure investment underway (federal infrastructure funds)
- Outage frequency has actually improved modestly by some metrics

**The fundamental problem:** Even if LUMA's defense is partially valid, Puerto Ricans are paying more for what they experience as the same bad service — politically toxic.

### Political Positions on LUMA

| Party | Position | Intensity |
|-------|---------|----------|
| PNP | Generally supports LUMA (they approved it); pragmatically defending | Medium |
| PPD | Critical; has called for renegotiating or canceling contract | High |
| MVC | Strongly against LUMA; wants return to public control | Very High |
| PIP | Strongly against LUMA; frames as colonial privatization | Very High |
| PD | Skeptical of government intervention; mixed | Medium |

**LUMA is the most universally disliked institution in Puerto Rico.** Even PNP politicians who officially support LUMA are careful about how much they associate with it.

## The Renewable Energy Mandate

Puerto Rico has a legally mandated renewable energy target:
- 100% renewable energy by 2050
- 40% by 2025, 60% by 2030 (these interim targets are significantly behind schedule)

### Solar Energy Boom
- Post-Maria: Tens of thousands of Puerto Rican households installed rooftop solar and battery storage
- This is partly economic (electricity is expensive) and partly trauma response (never again without power)
- Solar + battery is the most reliable electricity in PR — even when LUMA fails
- Constitutes a de facto decentralized energy revolution happening alongside LUMA's centralized system

### Community Microgrids
- Casa Pueblo in Adjuntas became world-famous for its solar microgrid that kept the community running for months during Maria blackouts
- Model being replicated in other communities
- Federal and state energy funds are supporting community microgrid development
