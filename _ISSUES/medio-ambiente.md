# Issue Intelligence: Medio Ambiente (Environment)

## Overview
Environmental issues in Puerto Rico intersect with colonial history, environmental justice for poor communities of color, the energy transition, hurricane vulnerability, and agricultural policy. The environment is not just a policy area — it is a narrative of what has been done to Puerto Rico and what Puerto Ricans are fighting to protect.

---

## The Petrochemical Complex (Peñuelas/Guayanilla)

### The Complex
The southwestern Puerto Rico coast near Peñuelas and Guayanilla hosts one of the Caribbean's largest petrochemical industrial zones:
- AES Puerto Rico: Coal-fired power plant (the only coal plant in PR)
- CIRO Industrial: Various petrochemical operations
- Fuel storage facilities
- Historically: Fertilizer plants, chemical manufacturing

### Health Concerns
- Communities surrounding these facilities have documented concerns about:
  - Air quality (particulate matter, sulfur dioxide from coal plant)
  - Coal ash disposal: AES has large coal ash deposits that community groups argue are contaminating groundwater and soil
  - Cancer rates: Some community health advocates report elevated cancer rates, though comprehensive studies are contested

### The Comité Dialo
A community environmental advocacy organization in Peñuelas that has fought against the petrochemical complex for decades. Their work represents Puerto Rico's environmental justice tradition: poor communities of color bearing the environmental burden of industrial activity that benefits others.

### AES Coal Plant — Political Issue
- The coal plant's contract has been a political battleground
- MVC/PIP/PPD: Oppose coal; want plant closed
- PNP: Has been more ambivalent (coal provides electricity price stability)
- PR's renewable energy mandate technically requires a path away from coal

## Coral Reef Crisis

- Puerto Rico's coral reefs have declined dramatically over recent decades
- **Causes:** Ocean warming (bleaching), disease (particularly stony coral tissue loss disease), runoff, development, recreational overuse
- **Impact:** Coral reefs protect coastlines from storms; support fisheries; attract tourism
- Loss of reefs = increased coastal erosion and vulnerability
- Environmental organizations are actively documenting reef damage and advocating for protection
- Political issue: Tourism industry (which benefits from reefs) vs. development that damages them

---

## Mangrove Loss

- Puerto Rico has lost significant mangrove coverage
- Mangroves protect coastlines, provide fish nurseries, store carbon
- Lost to coastal development, dredging, pollution
- Some restoration efforts underway but limited scale

---

## Solid Waste Crisis

- Puerto Rico's landfills are reaching capacity or have exceeded permitted capacity
- The island produces significant solid waste and recycling infrastructure is limited
- Some municipalities struggle to manage waste consistently
- Hurricane Maria produced enormous debris that strained the waste system
- Illegal dumping (vertederos ilegales) is a significant problem in rural areas
- Political issue: Waste management contracts are controversial (cost, effectiveness)

## Water Quality

### PRASA and Water Contamination
- Beyond service interruptions, PRASA has faced water quality concerns
- Some communities have detected contaminants in drinking water
- Infrastructure deterioration can introduce contaminants
- EPA oversight of PRASA includes water quality standards

### River and Groundwater
- Agricultural runoff affects river quality
- Industrial pollution near some water sources
- The Karst region of northern PR (a unique limestone geology) has fragile groundwater systems
- The Karst region is particularly important because it underlies the Arecibo/Norte area — Edgar Robles' district

### Superfund Sites
- Puerto Rico has multiple EPA Superfund sites (contaminated areas requiring federal cleanup)
- Vieques: Former Navy bombing range is a Superfund site with ongoing cleanup
- Various industrial sites across the island

## Climate Change Vulnerability

### Hurricane Risk
- Puerto Rico is in the Atlantic hurricane belt; climate change is intensifying storms
- Maria (2017) and Fiona (2022) demonstrated the island's catastrophic vulnerability
- Sea surface temperatures are rising, fueling stronger storms
- This is not abstract for Puerto Ricans — it is lived trauma

### Sea Level Rise
- PR's extensive coastline faces significant sea level rise risks
- Coastal infrastructure (roads, buildings, beaches) at long-term risk
- Low-lying communities (some in caseríos) at flood risk

### Drought
- PR faces increasing drought risk, particularly in the south (Ponce area)
- The 2021 drought was severe; water restrictions were implemented
- PRASA's water supply systems are vulnerable to drought

## Renewable Energy Transition

- PR's 100% renewable mandate by 2050 requires massive investment in solar, wind, and storage
- Progress has been slow relative to targets
- Rooftop solar boom: Puerto Ricans have installed solar at high rates (economic necessity + trauma response)
- Offshore wind: Potential for significant generation; studies underway
- Environmental advocates support the transition but also raise concerns about where renewable facilities are sited (land use conflicts)

## Casa Pueblo: The Model

Casa Pueblo in Adjuntas is Puerto Rico's most celebrated environmental organization and a model for the island:
- Founded by Alexis Massol González to oppose a copper mine in the 1970s
- Won the battle against the mine; transition to conservation and community development
- Installed a solar microgrid that kept the community running during Maria — became internationally famous
- Received the Goldman Environmental Prize (international recognition)
- Represents: Community-based environmental protection + renewable energy + dignity in disaster
- Politically: Associated with independence/progressive tradition

## Landing Page Implications

- Environment is most salient for urban (gentrification/nature connection) and coastal (hurricane vulnerability) constituents
- For interior mountain politicians (Rivera Segarra): Environmental issues = land, agriculture, river health, post-Maria recovery
- For coastal politicians: Coral reefs, flooding, beach access
- For Norte politicians (Robles, Brenda Pérez): Karst geology, PRASA water quality, Río Grande de Arecibo
- Issue dropdown: "Medio Ambiente" less commonly the #1 issue for most districts vs. water, jobs, electricity — but include it
- MVC/PIP politicians: Feature environmental justice prominently
- PNP politicians: Frame as responsible development and disaster resilience
