# Issue Intelligence: Economía y Empleo

## The Core Reality
Puerto Rico has experienced one of the most severe and prolonged economic crises of any U.S. jurisdiction in modern history. Understanding this crisis is essential for understanding every politician's messaging, every constituent's concerns, and why our services — helping politicians connect with economically stressed communities — are so valuable.

---

## Historical Context: The Economic Trajectory

### The Bootstrap Era (1947–1973): Manufacturing Miracle
Operation Bootstrap (Operación Manos a la Obra), launched under PPD Governor Muñoz Marín, transformed Puerto Rico from an agrarian poverty economy to an industrial manufacturing economy using federal tax exemptions (Section 936) to attract U.S. corporations. GDP grew at 6–7% annually for decades. Manufacturing became the engine of growth.

### Section 936 and the Pharmaceutical Economy (1976–1996)
Section 936 of the U.S. Internal Revenue Code exempted profits earned by U.S. subsidiaries in Puerto Rico from federal corporate tax. This made PR enormously attractive for high-profit, low-labor manufacturing — particularly pharmaceuticals. Companies like Pfizer, Abbott, Johnson & Johnson, Eli Lilly built major facilities in PR. By the 1990s, Puerto Rico was producing more than 10% of U.S. pharmaceutical exports.

### Section 936 Phase-Out (1996–2006): The Time Bomb
Congress eliminated Section 936 in 1996 (signed by President Clinton) over a 10-year phase-out period ending in 2006. This removed PR's primary tool for attracting and retaining investment. Companies began restructuring and reducing employment. The economic time bomb started ticking.

### The Fiscal Crisis (2006–2016): Debt Spiral
- Starting in 2006 (the year Section 936 fully ended): PR entered recession while the U.S. was still growing
- The government borrowed to maintain services rather than cut — debt accumulated
- By 2014: Puerto Rico's debt reached $72 billion (for an island of 3.3 million people)
- 2015: Governor García Padilla declared the debt "unpayable"
- 2016: PROMESA (Puerto Rico Oversight, Management, and Economic Stability Act) passed by U.S. Congress — imposed a Fiscal Oversight and Management Board ("La Junta")

### PROMESA and the Fiscal Board (2016–present)
**What the Fiscal Board is:**
- 7-member board appointed by U.S. Congress and President
- Controls Puerto Rico's budget — overrides the elected government if necessary
- Requires balanced budgets and debt payment plans
- Has negotiated with bondholders to restructure PR's debt
- By 2024: Has overseen restructuring of most of PR's debt

**Impact on Puerto Ricans:**
- Pension cuts for public employees
- Government worker layoffs
- University budget cuts (UPR)
- Reduced public services
- Seen by most Puerto Ricans as a colonial imposition; deeply unpopular

**Differing political positions:**
- PNP: Accepts the Board as necessary reality; focuses on working within it
- PPD: Critical but navigates it
- MVC/PIP: Demands dissolution of the Board; frames it as colonial control

### Hurricane Maria (2017): Catastrophe on Crisis
Hurricane Maria struck September 20, 2017 as a Category 4 storm:
- Destroyed 80% of Puerto Rico's electrical infrastructure
- 11 months without power for the last communities reconnected
- Official death toll: 2,975 people (excess mortality study)
- Infrastructure damage: $90+ billion
- Federal response: widely criticized as inadequate compared to mainland hurricane responses
- Population exodus: Estimated 135,000–200,000 people left PR after Maria

### Act 60 Era (2019–present): The Investor Controversy
Act 60 consolidated previous laws (Act 20 for export services, Act 22 for individual investors) into a comprehensive incentive package:
- Individual investors who move to PR: 0% capital gains tax on PR-sourced income
- Export services companies: 4% corporate tax rate
- Designed to attract investment and wealthy individuals
- **Controversy:** Has contributed to housing price inflation in San Juan/Santurce/Ocean Park; long-term residents priced out; community character changed
- **Divide:** PNP supports Act 60; MVC/PIP/progressive wing strongly oppose it

### The Brain Drain
Puerto Rico has lost approximately 700,000 people since 2000 — nearly 20% of its population. Those who leave tend to be younger, educated, and economically mobile. This creates a feedback loop: fewer working-age adults, smaller tax base, more dependency on federal programs, worse services, more emigration.

For EVERY politician's landing page:
- Economic pain is universal in PR — always include "economía y empleo" in the issue priority dropdown
