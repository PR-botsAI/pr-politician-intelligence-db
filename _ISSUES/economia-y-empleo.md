# Issue Intelligence: Economía y Empleo (Economy & Employment)

## Why This Issue Matters
Puerto Rico's economic crisis is the defining challenge of the 21st century for the island. Every politician must address it; none has fully solved it. This issue connects to federal policy, colonial status, PROMESA, and daily constituent survival.

---

## Puerto Rico's Economic Reality

### The Numbers (2024–2026)
- **GDP:** ~$115 billion (nominal) — but this significantly overstates living standards due to pharmaceutical profit repatriation
- **Poverty rate:** ~43–45% using federal poverty measure — highest of any U.S. state or territory
- **Unemployment:** ~6–8% official; underemployment and informal economy much higher
- **Population:** ~3.2 million — down from 3.8M in 2000; 600,000+ left
- **Median household income:** ~$22,000 — less than half of U.S. median
- **Food stamp (SNAP) dependency:** ~1.5M Puerto Ricans (about 47%) receive some form of nutrition assistance

### The Structural Problem
Puerto Rico's economy is structurally dependent on:
1. **Pharmaceutical manufacturing** — PR produces ~50% of U.S. pharmaceuticals; this industry is highly automated, generates massive profits, but creates relatively few local jobs vs. GDP contribution
2. **Federal transfer payments** — Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, SNAP, VA benefits keep the economy functioning
3. **Government employment** — government is historically one of the largest employers
4. **Tourism** — growing but fragile (post-Maria decline; recovery; now COVID; now recovery again)

### The Debt Crisis Context
- Puerto Rico accumulated ~$72 billion in public debt + $50 billion in pension obligations
- Declared effectively bankrupt in 2015 (García Padilla's "la deuda es impagable")
- PROMESA (2016): U.S. Congress imposed a Fiscal Oversight and Management Board (FOMB)
- FOMB has implemented austerity: public pension cuts, public employee layoffs, school closures, hospital closures
- FOMB is scheduled to remain until PR's fiscal plans are certified — ongoing

## The Act 60 (Investor Incentive) Controversy

### What It Is
Act 60 (formerly Act 20/22) provides massive tax incentives for wealthy individuals and businesses to relocate to Puerto Rico:
- **Individual investors:** 0% capital gains tax, 4% corporate tax rate
- **Export services businesses:** 4% tax rate on services exported from PR
- Designed to attract wealthy Americans to live in PR and invest

### The Case For
- Brings investment and economic activity
- Wealthy residents spend money locally
- Creates jobs in high-end services
- Diversifies economy

### The Case Against
- Puerto Ricans pay ~33% effective federal tax rate when earning on mainland; these investors pay 0%
- Drives up real estate prices, displacing Puerto Ricans from their neighborhoods
- Santurce, Río Grande, Rincón gentrification stories
- Creates resentment — "millionaires don't pay taxes while we suffer"
- Economic benefits don't trickle down to working class
- Mariana Nogales and MVC have made this their signature economic issue

## Employment Landscape

### Key Industries
1. **Pharmaceuticals:** Johnson & Johnson, Abbott, AbbVie, Pfizer, Amgen — hundreds of thousands of indirect jobs
2. **Government:** Federal + PR government — major employer
3. **Healthcare:** Hospitals, clinics, home health — large workforce
4. **Retail/commerce:** Major employer but low wages
5. **Tourism/hospitality:** Significant post-Maria recovery; seasonal
6. **Agriculture:** Small share but cultural importance; Hatillo dairy significant locally
7. **Technology/remote work:** Growing; pandemic accelerated

### The Brain Drain
PR loses highly educated professionals to the U.S. mainland:
- Doctors, nurses, engineers, accountants leave for higher wages
- University graduates leave immediately after graduating
- This depresses local human capital and healthcare quality
- "La fuga de cerebros" — the brain drain — is a crisis politicians reference constantly

## How to Use This Issue in Landing Pages

### For PNP Politicians
- Frame economic development through statehood lens (federal parity, voting rights, investments)
- Highlight federal funding secured for district
- Celebrate business openings, jobs created in district
- Acknowledge crisis honestly; blame structural causes, propose solutions

### For PPD Politicians
- Frame as protecting working families from austerity cuts
- Oppose LUMA/privatization as economic threat
- Highlight support for local business and workers
- Connect to Muñoz Marín legacy of building PR's economy

### For MVC/PIP Politicians
- Frame through decolonization — colonial relationship causes economic dependence
- Oppose Act 60 displacement directly
- Advocate living wages, worker protections, cooperatives
- Connect inequality to colonial structure
